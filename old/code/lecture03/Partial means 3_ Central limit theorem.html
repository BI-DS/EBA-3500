Partial means 3: Central limit theorem

Jonas Moss

Normal variables (i)

  • Let X1,X2∼N(μ,σ)X1,X2∼N(μ,σ), i.e., they are normal with expected value μμ and standard deviaton σσ.
  • Then aX+b∼N(aμ+b,aσ)aX+b∼N(aμ+b,aσ)
  • And X1+X2∼N(2μ,2–√σ)X1+X2∼N(2μ,2σ).
  • Task 1: Use these to show that ∑ni=1Xi∼N(nμ,n−−√σ).∑i=1nXi∼N(nμ,nσ).
  • Task 2: Use these to show that n−−√Mn−μσ∼N(0,1)nMn−μσ∼N(0,1).

Normal variables (ii)

  • The rescaled and recentered means n−−√Mn−μσnMn−μσ follow a standard normal distribution exactly.
  • By “standard normal” I mean N(0,1)N(0,1).
  • This is not true for any other random variables.

The central limit theorem (i)

  • The central limit theorem states, formally, that
    n−−√Mn−μσ→dN(0,1)nMn−μσ→dN(0,1)
  • Here “→d→d” denotes convergence in distribution.
  • Definition: Xn→dFXn→dF for a continuous FF if, when FnFn is the distribution of XnXn, the distribution function Fn(x)Fn(x) converges to F(x)F(x) pointwise.
  • But think about it in terms of approximations instead.

The central limit theorem (ii)

n−−√Mn−μσ→dN(0,1)nMn−μσ→dN(0,1)

  • You can expect the distribution of the rescaled means to be approximately normal provided nn is large enough.
  • What a large nn means varies, but n=30n=30 usually suffices for a solid approximation.
  • Recall that normal variables obey the central limit theorem exactly. But no other distribution does that.

Application

  • Let M10M10 be the sum of 10 uniformly distributed variables.
  • They have mean μ=1/2μ=1/2 and variance σ2=1/12σ2=1/12.
  • What is the approximate probability P(M10>3/4)P(M10>3/4)?
  • From the central limit theorem we have that Mn≈N(μ,σ/n−−√)Mn≈N(μ,σ/n) in distribution.
  • Task: Using the properties of the normal distribution and the central limit theorem, convince yourself that this is the case. (Hint: Divide, multiply, and subtract.)

Application (ii)

  • The CDF of a normal Z∼N(μ,σ)Z∼N(μ,σ) is denoted P(Z≤z)=Φ(z;μ,σ)P(Z≤z)=Φ(z;μ,σ).
  • Thus P(M10>3/4)=1−P(M10≤2)≈1−Φ(z;μ,σ)P(M10>3/4)=1−P(M10≤2)≈1−Φ(z;μ,σ).
